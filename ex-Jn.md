## Abstract

Bangladesh long saw falling poverty, but recent estimates show reversal. National poverty is projected near **27.9%** in 2025 (from **18.7%** in 2022); extreme poverty may rise from **5.6%** to about **9.3%**. The Gini climbed from **0.46** (2010) to **0.50** (2022). This paper uses Group 10’s presentation (*Poverty, Income Inequality and Social Safety Net Program in Bangladesh*, Nadia Akter et al., JKKNIU) plus cited sources. Safety nets are essential, yet durable progress also requires better jobs, sharper targeting, and growth that reaches the lower half of earners.

## 1. Introduction

Poverty and inequality are central to Bangladesh’s development debate, not minor indicators on a dashboard. A large share of the population still depends on agriculture, informal wage work, petty trade, or remittances from the Middle East and Southeast Asia. That structure makes households sensitive to shocks: a bad monsoon on chars, river erosion in coastal districts, a spell of double-digit food inflation, or a slowdown in garment orders can move a family from just above the poverty line to below it within months.

## 2. Conceptual Framework

**Poverty** means lacking income or resources for food, water, shelter, and healthcare at a minimum standard. *Absolute* poverty uses a fixed line; *relative* poverty compares households within the country.

**Extreme poverty** is inability to meet survival needs. The World Bank line is **$2.15/day** (2017 PPP). Bangladesh has reduced extreme poverty over decades, but it persists in rural and vulnerable areas. Group 10 cites a World Bank projection of **~15.8 million** in extreme poverty by end-2025.

**Income inequality** is the unequal spread of earnings. The **Gini** ranges from 0 (equal) to 1 (one recipient has all). Higher inequality can mean social tension, less schooling investment among the poor, and poverty across generations.

**SSNPs** are government cash and in-kind benefits. Outcomes depend on targeting, timely payment, benefits keeping pace with prices, and links to employment policy.

## 3. Poverty in Bangladesh

### 3.1 Causes

Group 10 groups causes into: (1) **environment and climate**—disasters and climate stress; (2) **economic challenges**—inflation, unemployment, inequality; (3) **social and health barriers**—population density, healthcare costs, lack of skills; (4) **structural issues**—infrastructure gaps and **regional disparity** (growth centred on Dhaka vs lagging western districts and haor areas).

### 3.2 Current Situation (2022 vs 2025)

| Indicator | 2022 | 2025 (est.) |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| National poverty | 18.7% | ~27.9% |
| Extreme poverty | 5.6% | ~9.3% |
| Vulnerable to poverty | — | ~62 million |

Rural poverty remains high, but **urban poverty is rising fast** (informal jobs, rents, migration without secure housing). Main drivers: **high inflation**, **weak job market**, **economic shocks**.

Between 2010 and 2022, the poverty rate fell by almost thirteen percentage points as remittances, exports, and agricultural output often performed strongly. The projected increase for 2025 interrupts that downward path. In econometric language, the series may show a **structural break**: regressors that once explained poverty reduction—relatively stable food prices, robust overseas employment—appear to matter less once inflationary pressure and labour-market weakness intensify. The presentation also notes a narrative of roughly **three million new extreme poor** associated with the 2025 projection, underlining that the reversal is not only a statistical artefact but a human one.

## 4. Income Inequality

### 4.1 Causes

Unequal **education**, a **skill gap** (formal jobs pay more than farm/casual work), **regional disparities**, unequal **land and assets**, and an **economic structure** favouring capital-heavy urban services over labour-intensive rural activity.

### 4.2 Current Distribution

- Gini: **0.46** (2010) → **0.50** (2022)  
- Top 10%: **~40.9%** of income; bottom 50%: **~19.05%**  
- Bottom decile: **1.31%** (2022) vs **2.58%** (1990–91)  
- Urban Gini **~0.54**; rural **~0.45**  
- Top 10% own **~58%** of wealth; top 1% **~24%**

**Figure 3.** Current inequality. **Figure 4.** Gini trend. **Figure 5.** Rural vs urban.  
![Figure 3](generated_graphs/figure3_income_inequality.png)  
![Figure 4](generated_graphs/figure4_gini_trend.png)  
![Figure 5](generated_graphs/figure5_rural_urban.png)

The Gini rise is fairly continuous rather than episodic. Group 10’s key insight is that **economic growth has been unevenly distributed**: sectors employing many low-income workers—agriculture, small retail, informal services—have expanded more slowly than finance, real estate, and parts of manufacturing. The richest decile now receives roughly twice the **national income share** of the entire bottom half, and **wealth inequality** (top 10% holding ~58% of wealth) exceeds income inequality because asset prices, especially land and housing, have outpaced wages.

Rural areas show **lower Gini (~0.45)** but **higher poverty incidence** because mean incomes are low and non-farm opportunities are limited; the economy still rests heavily on agriculture. Urban areas show **higher Gini (~0.54)**—more jobs, but also a wider gap between professionals and workers in informal sectors and slums. **Rapid urbanisation** in Dhaka and other centres without affordable housing and transport elevates both inequality and urban poverty risk, matching the presentation’s contrast between “more opportunities” and “uneven income distribution.”

## 5. Social Safety Net Programs

### 5.1 Major Programs

1. **Vulnerable Group Feeding (VGF)** — crisis food aid  
2. **Old Age Allowance**  
3. **Widow and Deserted Women Allowance**  
4. **Disability Allowance**  
5. **Primary Education Stipend** — attendance among poor families  

They cushion shocks but rarely end poverty without earned income.

### 5.2 Budget and Effectiveness

- **BDT 1,16,731 crore** (~USD 9.57 bn); **16.8%** of budget; **~3.01%** of GDP  
- **99** programmes (from 140); **39** pro-poor with **BDT 37,076 crore** for **7.68 crore** people  
- Life-cycle reform; **Tk 50–100** allowance hikes for elderly, widows, disabled  

**Figure 6.** SSNP budget allocation.  
![Figure 6](generated_graphs/figure6_budget_allocation.png)

Where implementation works, the slides credit SSNPs with **reducing extreme poverty** in selected areas, **improving food security**, providing **financial support in crises**, **raising school enrolment**, and **strengthening rural livelihoods**. Where delivery is weak, the same programmes face **targeting errors** (wrong households on lists), **benefit levels too low** relative to rising living costs, **leakage and corruption**, **irregular or delayed payments**, **limited coverage** compared with total need, and **weak monitoring with little transparency**. The gap between **7.68 crore** listed beneficiaries and **62 million** people still vulnerable to poverty suggests that nominal coverage does not equal effective protection after inflation and administrative loss.

## 6. Broader Impacts

Group 10 separates **structural** from **social** drivers of poverty and inequality. Structural factors include **landlessness** and unequal asset distribution, a large **informal labour market** without contracts or benefits, **climate change** and environmental shocks, **weak governance and corruption**, and **economic disparities** between regions. Social factors include **gender discrimination**, **educational disparities**, **demographic pressures**, and **sociocultural practices** that limit women’s mobility and earning power.

**Economic and social impacts** run in both directions. Low productivity among undernourished or poorly schooled workers slows broad-based growth; **extreme wealth concentration** dampens local demand; **unemployment** and **inflation eroding wages** squeeze the bottom of the distribution; **food insecurity** and barriers to essential services deepen hardship; and **inefficient safety nets** absorb budget without fully stabilising households.

**Human Development Index (HDI)** outcomes suffer even when money income is slightly above the poverty line. Poor children attend overcrowded schools, **drop out** early—especially in rural areas—and lose future employment chances. **Malnutrition**, limited healthcare access, and high out-of-pocket health spending hit mothers and infants hardest. **Inadequate housing, sanitation, clean water, and electricity** lower quality of life and pull down Bangladesh’s longer-run HDI trajectory alongside the Gini.

**COVID-19** added a sharp shock: **rise in poverty**, **employment crisis** in transport, retail, and garment auxiliaries, **education inequality** where online classes were impossible, and **health inequality** as savings were spent on treatment. GDP has recovered, but many households entered 2023–2025 with smaller buffers and worse job quality—just as food and rent inflation returned, amplifying the 2025 poverty projections in the slides.

## 8. Government Policies and Challenges

Government response, as outlined in the presentation, combines **Five-Year Plans** promising territorial balance, **consolidation of safety-net schemes** (from 140 toward 99 programmes), **incremental allowance increases**, and complementary spending on **infrastructure, education, health, and skills**. Reform toward a **life-cycle based** support system signals intent to match benefits to age and vulnerability stages.

Yet **challenges** remain extensive: **corruption and political linkage** in beneficiary selection; **low quality of education and skills** despite stipends; **rapid population growth**; **urban slums and housing crisis**; **targeting errors** and **limited coverage**; **low real funding per capita** after inflation; **weak monitoring and implementation**; **climate disasters**; **regional disparities**; and **rising cost of living**. The policy problem is often not the absence of programmes on paper but **implementation**—who appears on union lists, whether stipends improve learning or only enrolment, and whether headline GDP reaches informal and rural workers.

## 9. Recommendations and Conclusion

Following Group 10’s seven recommendations:

1. **Improve targeting** — household registry, NID linkage, community checks  
2. **Raise and index benefits** to food and essentials  
3. **Transparency** — publish funds released, beneficiaries paid, dates  
4. **Monitoring** — BBS or university follow-up surveys  
5. **Education and skills** — vocational training for garments, logistics, farming  
6. **Rural development** — irrigation, extension, roads  
7. **Inclusive growth** — jobs at living wages, not headline GDP alone  

**Conclusion:** Poverty fell from **31.5%** (2010) to **18.7%** (2022)—a real gain from remittances, garments, and harvests. By 2025 projections point to **~27.9%** national poverty and **~9.3%** extreme poverty. Gini **0.50** and a bottom-decile share of **1.31%** show uneven growth. SSNPs over **BDT one lakh crore** remain vital for floods, the elderly, and schooling, but without decent work and fair delivery they cannot fix inequality. The next **HIES** will likely confirm what Figures 1–7 suggest: averages improve while life at the bottom stalls or slips.
